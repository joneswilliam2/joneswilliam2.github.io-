A Look Into the Social and Economic Pressures of the Permian Basin

Will Jones · University of Houston · 2025

This paper examines why persistent social distress coexists with sustained economic growth in the Permian Basin, one of the most productive oil-producing regions in the United States. Using a panel dataset of 24 Texas counties spanning 2009 to 2023, combined with original IRB-approved qualitative fieldwork conducted in Odessa, Texas, I document a consistent decoupling between rising median household incomes and social outcomes including teen birth rates, drug overdose mortality, and educational attainment. A two-way fixed effects specification with county and year fixed effects identifies single-parent household share as the dominant within-county predictor of teen birth rates, while income shows no statistically significant independent effect. An instrumental variables specification using WTI crude oil prices interacted with a Permian Basin indicator confirms this null result. These findings suggest that resource-driven income gains do not translate automatically into human capital accumulation or social improvement, consistent with a place-based inequality framework in which structural and cultural barriers mediate the relationship between economic growth and wellbeing.

Do Macroeconomic Conditions Drive Crime? Evidence from Monthly U.S. Time Series Data, 2010–2025

Will Jones · University of Houston · 2026

This paper empirically investigates the relationship between macroeconomic conditions and national crime rates using monthly U.S. time series data from 2010 to 2025. Following Augmented Dickey-Fuller testing, all variables are first-differenced. The analysis estimates a baseline ARIMAX, a SARIMAX with monthly fixed effects and a pandemic structural break dummy, and a VAR(12) to address endogeneity and persistent seasonality. A joint Granger causality test fails to reject the null that unemployment and income do not predict crime (F = 1.30, p = 0.155). Approximately 88% of crime variance is attributable to its own innovations, suggesting that at the monthly frequency, the dominant driver of crime fluctuations is the series' own historical momentum rather than contemporaneous macroeconomic conditions. These findings do not invalidate Becker's (1968) theoretical framework but point to an important temporal limitation: rational behavioral responses to economic incentives likely unfold over quarters or years rather than months.

Energy Market Integration and Renewable Transition: A Vector Error Correction Approach

Will Jones · University of Houston

Examines the long-run cointegrating relationship between the iShares Global Clean Energy ETF (ICLN) and traditional energy benchmarks (WTI crude oil and Henry Hub natural gas) using Johansen cointegration tests and a vector error correction model (VECM). Finds evidence of a long-run equilibrium relationship between clean and traditional energy markets, with implications for the speed and nature of the energy transition.

Keywords: energy markets, cointegration, VECM, renewable energy, WTI, Henry Hub

Classifying Income Distributions Using Machine Learning: Evidence from Census Microdata

Will Jones · University of Houston

Applies K-nearest neighbors and support vector machine classifiers to U.S. Census microdata to predict household income categories from demographic and labor market characteristics. Evaluates model performance across classification thresholds and discusses implications for understanding the determinants of income stratification.

Keywords: machine learning, income classification, KNN, SVM, Census microdata
